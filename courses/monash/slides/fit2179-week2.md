# What Vis 1 Asks You To Build

- design and build an effective visualisation for a specific domain
- use real data that is relevant to that domain
- tell a story, not just show charts
- demonstrate Munzner's What, Why, and How framework

::right::

## Audience

Design for an average Australian or Malaysian, depending on your campus.

<div class="mt-6 border-l-2 border-slate-400 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
Use clear explanations, limited jargon, and visuals that do not assume expert statistical knowledge.
</div>

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Design direction</p>

# Presentation First, Not Exploration

- guide the reader through a clear story
- use text, annotations, layout, and typography deliberately
- add interactivity only where it genuinely helps
- avoid building a dashboard that feels like an expert analysis tool

::right::

## In practice

- sequence matters
- readability matters
- annotations matter
- good storytelling matters

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Non-negotiables</p>

# Core Requirements

- use accurate real-world data
- combine data from at least two sources
- design for an average Australian or Malaysian audience
- publish the work as one scrollable Tableau Public page

::right::

## Also required

- minimum of 10 charts
- maps are disregarded in marking for Vis 1
- avoid buttons that swap major sections of the page
- show storytelling, layout, typography, and annotation quality

<div class="mt-5 border-l-2 border-sky-700 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
This assignment is presentation-first. It is not meant to be an expert exploration tool.
</div>

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Integrity and attribution</p>

# Sources, Authorship, and AI

- credit your data sources and any external materials you use
- make sure you have the right to use images, icons, and other assets
- include your authorship and the date on the final work
- acknowledge any generative AI use in the submission

::right::

## Do not do this

- do not submit someone else's charts or infographic elements
- do not just tweak an existing Tableau design
- do not treat unattributed AI output as your own original material

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Marking priorities</p>

# What The Rubric Rewards

## Large mark areas

- idioms and complexity: `12%`
- storytelling, annotations, grammar, and style: `5%`
- layout, colour, and figure-ground: `4%`
- typography: `3%`

::right::

## Practical takeaway

- do not treat text and annotations as optional
- careful page structure matters
- for D or HD work, custom-built idioms matter
- metadata and clear authorship also count

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Topic scope</p>

# What Counts As A Viable Topic

## Strong direction

- specific domain, not a broad global theme
- clear audience and story angle
- multiple compatible datasets
- regional relevance to Australia or Malaysia
- real personal relevance or interest

::right::

## Weak topic choices

- Melbourne traffic accidents
- Sweden house prices
- topics you have no real connection to

<div class="mt-6 border-l-2 border-slate-400 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
A topic becomes stronger if you have clear personal relevance, background knowledge, or lived experience.
</div>

<div class="mt-5 border-l-2 border-slate-400 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
Specialised beats generic. Students should narrow early instead of trying to visualise everything.
</div>
