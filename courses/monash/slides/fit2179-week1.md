# Core Framework

Munzner’s What, Why, How model gives us a clean way to analyse visualisations before we build them.

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Visualisation theory</p>

# What, Why, How

## Munzner's visualisation design framework

<VClicks>

- **What** asks what data and attributes are present.
- **Why** asks what task the viewer is trying to perform.
- **How** asks what marks and channels encode the data.

</VClicks>

<div class="mt-6 border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
This is the analysis structure you will reuse in forum posts and project decisions.
</div>

---
layout: two-cols
---

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Framework 1</p>

# What: Data Abstraction

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Dataset types</strong></p>
  <v-clicks>
    <ul class="mt-3">
      <li>Tables: rows and columns</li>
      <li>Networks: nodes and links</li>
      <li>Fields: continuous spatial data</li>
      <li>Geometry: shapes and locations</li>
    </ul>
  </v-clicks>
</div>

::right::

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Attribute types</strong></p>

  | Type | Example |
  |------|---------|
  | Categorical | Country, gender |
  | Ordinal | Small, medium, large |
  | Quantitative | GDP, population |
</div>

<div class="mt-5 border-l-2 border-sky-700 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
Choosing a chart starts with understanding the structure and type of your data.
</div>

---
layout: two-cols
---

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Framework 2</p>

# Why: Task Abstraction

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Actions</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Analyze: consume or produce insights</li>
    <li>Search: lookup, locate, browse, explore</li>
    <li>Query: identify, compare, summarise</li>
  </ul>
</div>

::right::

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Targets</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>All data: pattern overview</li>
    <li>Attributes: variable relationships</li>
    <li>Network or spatial structure</li>
  </ul>

  <div class="mt-5 border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4">
    <p><strong>Example</strong></p>
    <p class="mt-2">Compare life expectancy across countries.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Framework 3</p>

# How: Marks and Channels

Visual encoding maps data to forms people can perceive and compare.

- marks define the geometric primitives
- channels define how values are perceived
- stronger encodings support more accurate comparison

<div class="mt-6 border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
This is the bridge between theory and what you actually build in Tableau.
</div>

# Forum Post Example

## How to write the analysis

<VClicks>

1. **What**: describe the dataset type and attribute types.
2. **Why**: explain the task, target, and analytical goal.
3. **How**: identify the marks and channels used in the chart.
4. **Critique**: state one strength and one limitation.

<div class="mt-5 border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
Checklist: What + Why + How + Critique + Story.
</div>

</VClicks>

---
layout: two-cols
---

<p class="text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-slate-500">Writing support</p>

# Forum Post Template

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm leading-relaxed">
  <p><strong>Template</strong></p>
  <blockquote class="mt-3">
    This visualisation shows <strong>[what data is shown]</strong>.<br /><br />
    <strong>What:</strong> The dataset is a <strong>[dataset type]</strong> with <strong>[attributes and types]</strong>.<br /><br />
    <strong>Why:</strong> The action is <strong>[task]</strong> on <strong>[target]</strong> to <strong>[goal]</strong>.<br /><br />
    <strong>How:</strong> It uses <strong>[marks]</strong> with <strong>[channels]</strong>.<br /><br />
    <strong>Critique:</strong> A strength is <strong>[advantage]</strong>. A limitation is <strong>[trade-off]</strong>.<br /><br />
    <strong>Story:</strong> The chart suggests <strong>[takeaway 1]</strong> and <strong>[takeaway 2]</strong>.
  </blockquote>
</div>

::right::

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm leading-relaxed">
  <p><strong>Example</strong></p>
  <p class="mt-3"><strong>What:</strong> A table dataset of country-year observations. Country is categorical, year is ordered temporal, and site count is quantitative.</p>
  <p class="mt-3"><strong>Why:</strong> Compare counts across countries and between 2004 and 2022, then summarise who leads and who changes most.</p>
  <p class="mt-3"><strong>How:</strong> Grouped bar marks use position and length on a common scale, while colour distinguishes countries.</p>
  <p class="mt-3"><strong>Critique:</strong> The grouped bars support comparison well, but they only show two time points.</p>
</div>

# Dimensions vs Measures

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Dimensions</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Usually text, dates, or geographic categories</li>
    <li>Used for grouping on Rows and Columns</li>
    <li>Also used on Colour, Shape, Label, and Filters</li>
    <li>Examples: Country Name, Year</li>
  </ul>
</div>

::right::

<div class="text-sm">
  <p><strong>Measures</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Numeric values that can be aggregated</li>
    <li>Often used for axes, size, and calculations</li>
    <li>Examples: Population as SUM or AVG</li>
  </ul>

  <div class="mt-5 border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4">
    <p><strong>Discussion question 2</strong></p>
    <p class="mt-2">Explore Dimensions, Measures, Filters, Marks, Columns, and Rows. What does each panel do?</p>
  </div>
</div>

# Wide vs Long Format

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Wide format</strong></p>

  | Country | 1960 | 1961 | 1962 |
  |---------|------|------|------|
  | China | 667M | 675M | 682M |
  | India | 450M | 460M | 470M |

  <p class="mt-3">Problem: years are spread across columns, so Tableau cannot easily treat year as a single field on an axis.</p>
</div>

::right::

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Long format</strong></p>

  | Country | Year | Population |
  |---------|------|------------|
  | China | 1960 | 667M |
  | China | 1961 | 675M |
  | India | 1960 | 450M |

  <p class="mt-3">Solution: one observation per row, with year stored as a proper variable.</p>
</div>

      <li>Drag Population to Rows.</li>
      <li>Add a Country filter and show the Year filter.</li>

Tip: green pills are continuous, blue pills are discrete.

# Continuous vs Discrete

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Discrete filter</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Uses tick-box selection</li>
    <li>Good for choosing specific years such as 1960 and 1980</li>
    <li>Shown as a blue pill</li>
  </ul>
</div>

::right::

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Continuous filter</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Uses a range slider</li>
    <li>Good for selecting intervals such as 1960 to 1980</li>
    <li>Shown as a green pill</li>
  </ul>
</div>

# Evaluating the Stacked Bar Chart

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Advantages</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Shows total population per country.</li>
    <li>Shows composition by year.</li>
    <li>Makes overall size differences visible.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

::right::

<div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
  <p><strong>Disadvantages</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-3">
    <li>Hard to compare the same year across countries.</li>
    <li>Too many colours can overload attention.</li>
    <li>The chart may not fit the story if the real goal is time-series change.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="mt-5 border-l-2 border-sky-700 pl-4 text-sm text-slate-600">
Discussion question 5: when does this design help, and when does it get in the way?

# Marks and Channels in Tableau

<div class="grid grid-cols-[1.4fr_1fr] gap-6 items-start">
  <div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
    <p><strong>Common mappings</strong></p>
    <ul class="mt-3">
      <li>Change mark type: Marks card to Bar, Line, Circle, or Area</li>
      <li>Use position: drag fields to Rows and Columns</li>
      <li>Use colour: drag a field to Colour</li>
      <li>Use size: drag a field to Size</li>
      <li>Use labels: drag a field to Label</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div class="border border-slate-300 bg-white/60 p-4 text-sm">
    <p><strong>Key takeaway</strong></p>
    <p class="mt-2">Tableau operationalises the <strong>How</strong> in visualisation design.</p>
    <ul class="mt-3">
      <li>Marks become bars, lines, circles, and areas.</li>
      <li>Channels become position, colour, size, and labels.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
